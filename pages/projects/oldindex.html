MARTHA Speaks: Implementing Theory of Mind for More Intuitive Communicative Acts

During the summer of 2015, I worked on a research project at the University of Illinois at Chicago (UIC) Artifical Intelligence Lab. Our goal was to develop an intelligent agent embued with a "theory of mind." Theory of Mind is an important human ability to imagine what others are thinking as one observes and interacts with another. Examples include keeping track of comprehension during a conversation, understanding unspoken queues and reading into subtext, or playing a competitive game.

The result, progammed in Java and Lisp, was MARTHA, the Mental-state Aware Real-time THinking Assistant, which, with further development, could compete with popular AI assistants like Siri and Google Now with its deep understanding of user desires. We wrote a paper describing its Theory of Mind algorithm, which we published in the proceedings of the 2015 AAAI Fall Symposia Series. Since it was accepted, I had the opportunity to travel to Arlington, Virgina to present my work to the premier researchers in AI...all as a high-school sophomore/junior!

Mark OvBot | A Silly Chatbot built with Markov Chains

Mark OvBot uses Markov Chains to model language and generate proper-sounding sentences. In the beginning, I trained Mark with curated corpuses, but I needed much more data in order to make it truely sophisticated. The next source was Twitter, but the content was choppy and full of links and emoji. Now, I am working on plugging Mark into news feeds so that it can learn from large edited bodies of proper english.

Mark differs from a naive implementations of Markov chatbots in that I've implemented a relevancy function into its code. Each time it generates a response, it creates a large number of candidate sentences. These are rated on how relevant the constituent words are to the seed query, and the highest rated response is the one that is presented to the user.

Mark is currently under testing in the wild. However, the content has been learned from unfiltered Tweets, such that it is not safe to link from this website.

HydroHero | Making Water for the People...from Thin Air!

Even in the driest deserts, there are vast amounts of water scattered in the air as water vapor. Anyone who’s had a ice cold drink in drier climates has experienced this through the condensation that appears on the glass.

HydroHero uses water condensation technology to extract this water from the air. As a personal water generator presented in one easy-to-use package, it seeks to provide peace-of-mind to those afflicted by a natural disaster or living in remote (or distitute) areas where there may not be electricity, gas, nor a central water supply.

Its principle of operation is intentionally simple: using solar energy, a fan draws water-laden air through a particulate filter (to remove dust) and into an array of fins chilled by a peltier tile. Meeting a surface cooler than the dew point, the water condenses into droplets, which then roll down the smooth surface of the fins, into a collecting funnel. From there, it exits the apparatus and accumulates in the collecting bin.

I've begun to experiment with prototypes built from common computer parts (a heatsink and a fan) and cheap Peltier tiles. In my early tests, I was able to see condensation appearing on the fins of a smaller heatsink! But to drive the large heatsink shown, I figure that I'll need more peltier tiles, and more energy.

Where there is water, there is life. HydroHero seeks to improve life by giving water to the people.

The IMSA Knowledge Exchange Network (IKEN) | Democratizing the Internet...one mesh network at a time

It is unnerving that Comcast holds a virtual monopoly over internet service in my area. Not only does the entire neighborhood suffer when there is an outage, due to the lack of competition, they have no barrier against charging exorbitant prices and providing poor service.

So I wondered, could there be a way to create a high-availability network that puts control of the internet into the hands of the people who frequent it, instead of the backbone providers? That's when IKEN was born, an idea place nodes in a "mesh network" with many possible routes from one node to another. This way, no one entity can control the internet, nor does a failure at one node result in a catastrophic failure systemwide.

I intend to test this as a messaging system locally at my school, which is why it's called the IMSA Knowledge Exchange Network.

The Super Computing at IMSA NETwork (SCAINET) | Providing high-performance computing with retired laptops

SCAINET started even before I had the idea to build it for IMSA. Around middle school, my parents' companies were downsizing/renewing their assets, so we got ahold of a couple of old desktops and servers for free (the same ones that now run Mark). Sure, they were fun to play with individually, but there were so many of them...I began to wonder if there was a way to combine their power. Back then, the frameworks available to the enthusiast were fairly primitive, but I got as far as writing a parallel C++ program with OpenMPI to (slowly and less efficiently) factor numbers.

Fast forward to 2014, my sophomore year and my first year at IMSA. I learned that there were a large number of modern laptops up for retirement soon, so I inquired with our IT services if I might acquire them. I did, and I put them together to make SCAINET. With the explosion of "cloud computing" in about 2012, there were now plenty of clustering frameworks, including dispy. With SCAINET, I rendered a 10000x10000px Mandelbrot set, helped peers generete 3D art and animations, and crunched through student simulations for their SIR projects. I am now upgrading SCAINET to become a more permanent and modular appliance for the school.
